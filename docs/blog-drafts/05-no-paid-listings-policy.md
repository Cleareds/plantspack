# Why we don't take paid listings (and what that costs us)

The most common email I get from restaurant owners is some version of:

> Hi! Can we pay to have our place featured on PlantsPack? We'd
> love to be at the top of [city] / get a "verified" badge / appear
> in your homepage carousel.

The answer is no. It's always no. It's structurally no, because the
moment we say yes to any of those, the rest of the platform loses
the only thing that makes it different.

Here's the full position.

## What paid listings would look like on a vegan directory

The standard model on competitor platforms looks like this:

- **"Featured" placement** at the top of city search results.
  Pays $30-$200/month per city per restaurant.
- **"Verified" or "Premium" badges** sold as upgrades, even
  when the venue hasn't been independently checked.
- **Banner ads** on search results pages.
- **Boosted listings** that ride higher when you don't pay.
- **Affiliate booking links** that pay the directory a cut of
  every reservation.

Each of these makes economic sense for the directory. Each of them
quietly corrodes the trust that vegans are using the directory to
solve. If the top-ranked place is the one that paid the most, the
ranking is no longer about vegan-friendliness — it's about marketing
budgets.

We've decided not to do any of those things. Not at $0, not at $50,
not at $50,000. The rule is in our code review checklist: any change
that affects ranking, badging, or placement is automatically denied
if it correlates with payment.

## What that costs us

The honest version: a lot, financially.

A directory like ours with our traffic could probably take in
**€500-2,000/month** at this scale from paid placements alone if
we set up the machinery. Within 18 months at 10× our current
traffic, that figure could be **€20K-50K/month**. That's lifestyle-
business money we're choosing not to take.

What we lose:
1. The fastest path to financial sustainability.
2. The ability to onboard a paid editor faster.
3. The ability to invest in a mobile app or more aggressive
   coverage expansion.

These are real costs. We are deliberately leaving them on the table.

## What we get instead

Three things, and they all compound:

### 1. Trust from vegans

The first thing a vegan traveller checks when arriving in a new city
is whether the recommendations they're getting are actually vegan or
just marketed at them. If the top of our Berlin list is whoever
paid the most, that check fails. If it's the genuinely highest-rated
fully-vegan place in Berlin, the check passes — and we get to keep
that vegan as a user for the next 10 years.

### 2. Trust from restaurants we exclude

This part is counter-intuitive. Restaurants we *don't* feature also
trust us more in the long run. When we say "we won't take your
money", they understand the playing field is level. The vegan place
across the street isn't paying to leapfrog them. They might not
like being mid-ranked, but they understand the ranking.

### 3. Trust from supporters

This is the one that matters financially. The supporter model
(€3/month, no perks gated by money beyond a badge + voting) only
works if supporters know their money isn't subsidising a separate
revenue stream we'd take anyway. The €3 is the whole thing. That's
why we publish supporter counts, why we don't run ads, and why the
"no paid listings, ever" line is on the homepage.

## How we keep the lights on

The current revenue mix:
- **Monthly supporters** at €3/month
- **Buy-Me-a-Coffee** one-time donations
- **Founder time**, unpaid

That's the whole list. There's no advertising revenue, no affiliate
revenue, no sponsorship revenue, no paid listings revenue, no
data-selling revenue. The supporter wall on `/support` shows every
recurring supporter (with their permission). It's all we have, and
it's working — slowly, but it's working.

## The values bet

The underlying bet is that **trust + audience + time** beats **paid
listings + churn**. We're betting that a smaller, honestly-ranked
directory with 1,000 loyal supporters is more durable than a bigger
one with 100,000 ambivalent users who don't trust the rankings.

I genuinely don't know yet if we're right. The model is still
proving itself. But after a year of running both ways in my head,
I'd rather take a longer path that builds a thing I'd actually use
myself than take the fast path and end up running a platform I
wouldn't recommend to a friend.

If that resonates, **[support the work →](/support)**. If not, the
directory's still free for you anyway. That part doesn't change.
